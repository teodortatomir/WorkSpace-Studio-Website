Workspace Strategy

Workplace research applied to your office.

Space analysis, organisational mapping, stakeholder alignment and a written project definition. Anchored in MillerKnoll workplace research and global studies, calibrated for your team and your floor.

Workplace Strategy is the diagnostic work that happens before architectural design begins. Workspace Studio runs space analysis, organisational mapping, stakeholder alignment and a written project definition that translates business priorities into a brief the design and engineering teams can build against.

The output is a written articulation of how the organisation works in practice, which spaces support which activities, where the friction currently sits, and what the office needs to deliver for engagement, retention and return-to-office adoption.

The work draws on MillerKnoll workplace research, global workplace studies, post-occupancy observations and our own delivery experience across 500,000 m² of corporate fit-out in Romania.

Workplace Strategy is offered either as a standalone consultancy engagement, or built into a Design & Build project as the first phase.

What workplace strategy reveals

Workplace strategy work translates abstract business priorities into concrete design requirements.

The questions Workspace Studio diagnoses for clients fall across three categories.

Operational priorities.

How does the organisation actually work day to day. Which teams collaborate, which roles need quiet, where people meet informally, what gets scheduled and what happens spontaneously. The answers shape the proportion of focused versus collaborative space, the number of meeting rooms by size, and the placement of social zones.

Headcount projections.

Realistic growth, restructuring, hybrid attendance patterns and seasonal variations determine how much space is enough, where flexibility needs to be built in, and which areas should be designed as reconfigurable from the start. Static space planning for a five-year lease while the company is doubling headcount is a frequent cause of expensive mid-lease moves and unplanned subleasing.

Engagement and return-to-office outcomes.

When the brief includes objectives for in-office attendance, employee engagement scores, retention or hybrid adoption, design decisions have to support those outcomes specifically. Workplace strategy turns those objectives into the brief that architects and engineers build against, with measurable post-occupancy metrics where useful.

Process

The four phases of a Workplace Strategy engagement

Space analysis.

We assess the existing office (or the new floor plate, for relocations): how it's used today, where the friction is, which zones are over- or under-utilised, what the current density and circulation look like. Observation, utilisation data where available, walk-throughs, occupancy sampling.

Understanding organisational goals, workflows and culture.

Working sessions with leadership, HR, facilities and a cross-section of the team. We map operational priorities, growth projections, hybrid working policies, team structures and the cultural elements that the office is meant to support.

Identifying operational challenges and transformation opportunities.

The gap between how the organisation works today and where it needs to go in 3-5 years. What the current office gets in the way of, what it could enable if redesigned, which behaviours the design should support versus discourage.

Project definition and stakeholder alignment.

A written project brief that consolidates the diagnostic work, formalises decisions, and aligns leadership, HR, facilities and procurement before architectural work begins. This is the deliverable that architects, engineers, cost teams and Workspace Studio build the project against.

When to consider workplace strategy

When clients engage Workspace Studio for workplace strategy

Common triggers fall across a few patterns.

New lease, new floor plate.

When the organisation is planning a relocation or expansion into a new floor, workplace strategy work happens before architects start drawing layouts. The brief includes headcount projections, working patterns and the constraints of the new building.

Hybrid working transition.

When in-office attendance changes substantially (downsizing space after permanent hybrid policy, or rebuilding space to support return-to-office adoption), the existing office no longer matches how people work. Workplace strategy maps the new patterns and translates them into a redesign brief.

Restructuring or rapid growth.

When teams change shape, through M&A, layoffs, doubling headcount, or shifting between functional and matrix structures, the office that worked for the old organisation creates friction for the new one. Workplace strategy identifies what to keep, what to reconfigure and what to rebuild.

Engagement or retention concerns.

When HR data shows declining engagement scores, rising attrition or hybrid attendance below target, the workplace can be diagnosed as a contributing factor. Workplace strategy isolates the office variables and recommends design changes that support measurable outcomes.

Pre-investment justification.

When a CFO or board requires business-case validation before approving an office investment, workplace strategy produces the diagnostic and projection work that builds the case.

Standalone or built-in

How Workplace Strategy is contracted

Workspace Studio offers Workplace Strategy in two distinct configurations.

As a standalone consultancy engagement.

Independent diagnostic work with no commitment to architectural design or fit-out execution by Workspace Studio. Output is a written brief that the client can take to any designer or general contractor, or use internally to clarify direction before going to market.

As the first phase of a Design & Build project.

Integrated into a full delivery contract, with the diagnostic work feeding directly into the design and execution phases handled by the same team. Faster and tighter because the strategists, architects and engineers work continuously across phases.
