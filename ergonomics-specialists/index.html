Ergonomics

Physical, social and inclusive ergonomics, integrated into every project.

Ergonomic work stations, environmental conditions, social design of space and inclusive considerations for the full diversity of a modern workforce.

During the over 15 years of close work as Herman Miller representatives in Romania, ergonomics has become part of the DNA of every Workspace Studio project.

The scope of ergonomics has expanded over time, from the individual workstation toward the wider physical environment, the social design of how people work together and the inclusive design philosophies that recognise the full diversity of a modern workforce.

What is office ergonomics

Designing the corporate workplace around the people who use it.

Office ergonomics is the science of designing the corporate workplace around the people who use it. The term ergonomics comes from the Greek words ergon (work) and nomos (laws or rules), and the discipline studies how the physical setup of the workstation, the sensory environment, the social organisation of space and the individual differences across a workforce affect performance, health, engagement and long-term wellbeing at work.

The International Organization for Standardization defines ergonomics in ISO 6385 as "the scientific discipline concerned with the understanding of interactions among humans and other elements of a system, and the profession that applies theory, principles, data and methods to design in order to optimise human well-being and overall system performance."

In the office context, this translates into the ergonomic design of workstations, the conditions of the wider environment in which they sit, the spatial layout that supports both collaboration and focused work, and the inclusive considerations that ensure the space functions for everyone who uses it.

Why ergonomics matters in the workplace

Retention, productivity, cognitive performance and wellbeing belong in the same conversation.

Office ergonomics is often treated as a health-and-safety line item, separate from business strategy. Recent research from Gensler, Gallup and Workplace Performance Index data shows it sitting at the centre of how organisations retain talent, engage employees and convert workplace investment into productivity.

Productivity.

Gensler's 2025 Global Workplace Survey, based on 16,809 full-time office workers across 15 countries, found that employees in great workplaces are nearly 3x more likely to stay with their company. 90% of employees who like their workspace report being proud to work for their company, compared with 47% among those who feel disconnected from the environment.

Better retention and lower absenteeism.

The same Gensler 2025 survey found that employees with high choice in where and how they work are 2.5x more likely to say their workplace supports both individual and team productivity. Spaces that provide control over lighting, noise, privacy and flexibility lead the way.

Cognitive performance.

Sustained attention, error rates, decision quality and the speed at which people recover from interruptions are all influenced by physical comfort, sensory environment, the absence of low-grade discomfort that accumulates over an 8-hour day, and the social conditions that surround the individual workstation. The workforce that performs at its cognitive ceiling sits in a workspace that has been designed for the body and the mind that occupies it.

Health and wellbeing.

Lower musculoskeletal strain, reduced eye fatigue, fewer headaches, lower stress-related symptoms. The same factors that produce performance gains produce health outcomes. The two are inseparable.

Physical ergonomics: the workstation

The individual workstation is where the largest measurable effects on daily wellbeing happen.

Most office workers spend 6 to 9 hours a day at the same setup. The cumulative effect of small ergonomic decisions compounds over weeks and years.

The chair. Ergonomic seating distributes weight across the body, supports the lumbar curve, allows the user to shift posture throughout the day and accommodates different body types. As MillerKnoll Certified Dealer in Romania, Workspace Studio anchors its seating offer around Herman Miller models that have defined contemporary office ergonomics: the iconic Aeron with its pioneering suspension and PostureFit support, the Embody designed around the spine itself, the Mirra 2 with its dynamic backrest and the Cosm with passive matching of body movement.

The desk. Height-adjustable workstations let the user alternate between sitting and standing across the workday, reducing the continuous spinal compression that fixed-height desks generate. Adjustment range, stability under load, control mechanism reliability and build quality determine whether the desk operates smoothly across many years of daily use or develops issues that compromise the ergonomic benefit.

The monitor and accessories. Monitor arms position the screen at eye level and at the correct distance, reducing neck strain and visual fatigue. Laptop stands raise the screen above the keyboard, eliminating the worst posture in modern office work: looking down at a laptop for 8 hours. External keyboards and mice complete the setup when laptops are the primary device.

The accumulating effect. Each of these elements alone produces a marginal improvement. Together, they shift the workstation from a setup that the body tolerates to one that supports sustained focus across the working day.

The difference is visible in posture observation, self-reported discomfort, the simple absence of end-of-day fatigue and the long-term protection against musculoskeletal strain that poorly designed setups produce.

Physical ergonomics: the office environment

Beyond the workstation, physical ergonomics extends into the conditions that surround it.

Lighting.

Natural light access, glare control and calibrated artificial lighting shape visual comfort and circadian rhythm. Get them wrong and people leave with eye fatigue, headaches and the mid-afternoon focus dip that no amount of coffee fixes. Specification covers facade orientation, glazing, shading strategy and the dimming control of artificial lighting.

Thermal comfort.

Temperature, humidity and air movement act directly on cognitive performance, and the comfortable range is narrower than most people expect. A floor running on a single setpoint will always leave some people too cold and others too warm. HVAC design with zoning and local control solves what one thermostat cannot.

Acoustics.

Noise is the single largest complaint in workplace surveys, and open-plan offices generate it by design. Acoustic specification covers ceiling and wall absorption, partition performance, the distance between meeting rooms and focused-work zones, and office pods for calls that need privacy.

Air quality.

Air quality affects cognition more than almost any other environmental factor, and it is the one people notice least. A 2023 systematic review in Building and Environment confirmed that CO2 above 1000 ppm measurably impairs complex cognitive tasks. Ventilation rates, filtration, low-VOC finishes and planting all keep the air on the right side of that threshold.

Social ergonomics: connection by design

Connection at work is engineered deliberately into the floor plan.

Physical ergonomics looks after the body. Social ergonomics looks after how people connect, collaborate and work alongside each other, and connection at work is something you engineer deliberately into the floor plan.

An office filled with open desks and a token coffee corner does not automatically produce collaboration. Just as often it produces noise, distraction, interruptions and the kind of low-grade friction that damages focused work, while the conversations that actually matter happen somewhere else or never happen at all. The difference between a floor that supports connection and one that undermines it comes down to deliberate design.

Good social ergonomics puts the right settings in the right places. Cafes and informal zones where cross-team conversations form by chance, the casual encounters that strengthen the looser professional relationships research links to innovation and knowledge flow. Connecting spaces for quick, unscheduled collaboration. Meeting rooms and focused zones positioned so that collaboration does not bleed into concentration. The aim is a floor where the right interactions become more likely and the wrong interruptions become rarer.

Designed well, social ergonomics raises collaboration and focus at the same time. Designed carelessly, an office trades one against the other and tends to lose both.

Inclusive ergonomics: design that works for everyone

Real workforces include different bodies, sensory profiles, cultural expectations and life stages.

Standard ergonomic guidance was built around an "average" worker who does not exist. Real workforces include different bodies, different sensory profiles, different cultural expectations and different life stages. Inclusive ergonomics designs for that range from the start, so the office works for the people who actually use it.

Neurodiversity.

An estimated 15-20% of people are neurodivergent, with different needs around sensory input, focus, predictability and stimulation. Inclusive design offers a spectrum of settings, from low-stimulation quiet rooms to enclosed focus pods, so people can match the environment to how their attention works.

Accessibility.

Step-free routes, accessible workstations, clear sightlines, tactile and visual wayfinding and adjustable furniture make the office usable for people with mobility, vision, hearing or other physical differences. Accessibility belongs in the brief from day one.

Families and new parents.

Returning to the office is particularly hard for people with young children. Inclusive design eases that transition with dedicated lactation and wellness rooms for new mothers, family rooms for the days when childcare falls through, parent-friendly quiet spaces and the practical provisions that make a working day workable under pressure. Small interventions, large effect on whether parents stay and return.

Cultural differences.

Expectations around privacy, hierarchy, prayer or quiet space and the balance between individual and collective work vary across cultures. International workforces need a range of settings rather than a single imposed norm.

Age differences.

A workforce spanning four generations has a wide range of needs around lighting levels, acoustic clarity, screen legibility and physical comfort. Designing for the older end of the range tends to improve the experience for everyone.
