Ergonomics

Physical ergonomics: the workstation

The individual workstation is where physical ergonomics starts and where the largest measurable effects on daily wellbeing happen.

The workstation

The individual workstation is where physical ergonomics starts and where the largest measurable effects on daily wellbeing happen. Most office workers spend 6 to 9 hours a day at the same setup. The cumulative effect of small ergonomic decisions compounds over weeks and years.

The chair. Ergonomic seating distributes weight across the body, supports the lumbar curve, allows the user to shift posture throughout the day and accommodates different body types. As MillerKnoll Certified Dealer in Romania, Workspace Studio anchors its seating offer around Herman Miller models that have defined contemporary office ergonomics: the iconic Aeron with its pioneering suspension and PostureFit support, the Embody designed around the spine itself, the Mirra 2 with its dynamic backrest and the Cosm with passive matching of body movement.

The desk. Height-adjustable workstations let the user alternate between sitting and standing across the workday, reducing the continuous spinal compression that fixed-height desks generate. Adjustment range, stability under load, control mechanism reliability and build quality determine whether the desk operates smoothly across many years of daily use or develops issues that compromise the ergonomic benefit.

The monitor and accessories. Monitor arms position the screen at eye level and at the correct distance, reducing neck strain and visual fatigue. Laptop stands raise the screen above the keyboard, eliminating the worst posture in modern office work: looking down at a laptop for 8 hours. External keyboards and mice complete the setup when laptops are the primary device.

The accumulating effect. Each of these elements alone produces a marginal improvement. Together, they shift the workstation from a setup that the body tolerates to one that supports sustained focus across the working day.

The difference is visible in posture observation, self-reported discomfort, the simple absence of end-of-day fatigue and the long-term protection against musculoskeletal strain that poorly designed setups produce.

Physical ergonomics: the office environment

Beyond the workstation, physical ergonomics extends into the conditions that surround it.

Beyond the workstation, physical ergonomics extends into the conditions that surround it. These are often invisible until they go wrong, and their cumulative effect across a workday is larger than any single adjustment at the desk.

Lighting.

Natural light access, glare control and calibrated artificial lighting shape visual comfort and circadian rhythm. Get them wrong and people leave with eye fatigue, headaches and the mid-afternoon focus dip that no amount of coffee fixes. Specification covers facade orientation, glazing, shading strategy and the dimming control of artificial lighting.

Thermal comfort.

Temperature, humidity and air movement act directly on cognitive performance, and the comfortable range is narrower than most people expect. A floor running on a single setpoint will always leave some people too cold and others too warm. HVAC design with zoning and local control solves what one thermostat cannot.

Acoustics.

Noise is the single largest complaint in workplace surveys, and open-plan offices generate it by design. Acoustic specification covers ceiling and wall absorption, partition performance, the distance between meeting rooms and focused-work zones, and office pods for calls that need privacy.

Air quality.

Air quality affects cognition more than almost any other environmental factor, and it is the one people notice least. A 2023 systematic review in Building and Environment confirmed that CO2 above 1000 ppm measurably impairs complex cognitive tasks. Ventilation rates, filtration, low-VOC finishes and planting all keep the air on the right side of that threshold.
